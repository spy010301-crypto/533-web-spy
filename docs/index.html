Systematic trend-following on SCHD (Schwab U.S. Dividend Equity ETF)

Author

Peiyuan Shan

Donchian Channel Breakout on SCHD

A transparent, rules-based trend-following system applied to a high-quality dividend-growth ETF: enter on 20-bar price breakouts, size exits with ATR, cap risk with a hard time-out.

Strategy Logic

The strategy watches a rolling 20-day high/low envelope — the classic Donchian channel — and waits for price to decisively pierce one of the boundaries. A close above the upper band signals that recent resistance has broken, the path of least resistance is up, and we go long the next morning; a close below the lower band does the symmetric thing on the short side. Every trade carries a pre-computed profit target of two ATRs and a stop-loss of one ATR from the entry, giving a 2:1 reward-to-risk geometry; if neither boundary is touched within twenty trading days the position is closed at the market so capital never sits idle in a range-bound trade. Only one position is open at a time, slippage and commission are charged on both legs, and no pyramiding is permitted.

Asset Selection

The candidate universe was an 8-ETF basket spanning equities, bonds, dividends, and commodities: SPY, QQQ, VIG, SCHD, TLT, GLD, XLE, and EEM. For each name we counted how many 20-bar Donchian breakouts occurred over a 3-year sample, then filtered for liquidity and trend persistence. SCHD was chosen as the strategy target for three reasons:

  1. Dividend-growth quality. SCHD tracks the Dow Jones U.S. Dividend 100 Index, which selects companies that have paid dividends for at least ten consecutive years and screens aggressively for cash-flow-to-debt, ROE, dividend yield, and 5-year dividend growth. The result is a book of mature, cash-generative businesses — the kind whose prices tend to trend smoothly rather than whipsaw, which suits a breakout system.
  2. Clean trending behaviour. Compared with higher-beta sector ETFs, SCHD shows fewer false breakouts and more sustained follow-through after the channel is breached. That is exactly the regime where a Donchian system shines: it sits out chop and rides the big directional moves.
  3. Deep liquidity & tight spreads. SCHD is one of the most widely held dividend ETFs; average daily volume runs in the millions of shares, so the 0.1% slippage assumption used below is conservative rather than optimistic.

Note that the strategy here is price-only — dividends are stripped out of the return series. This is the right comparison for a breakout system (we’re testing whether the price move is predictable), but it understates total-return performance that a real holder of SCHD would collect.

Breakout Definition

A breakout is defined mechanically so there is zero discretion:

  • Upper band = highest high of the prior 20 bars.
  • Lower band = lowest low of the prior 20 bars.
  • Long signal fires when today’s close > upper band; the trade is filled at the next bar’s open.
  • Short signal fires when today’s close < lower band; filled at the next bar’s open.
  • ATR uses Wilder smoothing over 14 bars.
  • Profit target = entry ± 2.0 × ATR.
  • Stop-loss = entry ∓ 1.0 × ATR.
  • Time-out = force-close at the close of bar 20 after entry.
  • Size = 100 shares per trade.
  • Costs = 0.1% slippage + 0.1% commission applied on both entry and exit sides.
  • Risk-free rate = 5.3% annualised, used for Sharpe / Sortino.

All channel values are shifted by one bar so only prior-bar information is ever used — no peek-ahead.

Trade Outcome Analysis

Every position is forced to close through one of three doors: the profit target (entry ± 2 × ATR), the stop-loss (entry ∓ 1 × ATR), or a time-out after 20 trading days. The stop-loss is tested each bar against the bar’s low (for longs) or high (for shorts); if the stop and the target are both reachable on the same bar, the stop is assumed to fill first — a conservative choice that avoids flattering the numbers.

Assumptions

  • Slippage: 0.1% of fill price applied on every entry and exit.
  • Commission: 0.1% per side, in addition to slippage.
  • No pyramiding: only one position is held at any time; new signals during an open trade are ignored.
  • Data source: live SCHD daily bars from Interactive Brokers TWS (ib_insync); if TWS is unreachable the pipeline falls back to a synthetic GBM series calibrated to SCHD-like parameters (price ~$27.50 base, ~8% drift, ~16% vol) so the site always renders.
  • No dividends: SCHD quarterly distributions are ignored; returns are price-only. For a buy-and-hold comparison the total-return picture would be meaningfully better, but the breakout signal is defined on price so a price-only test is the right benchmark for this strategy.
  • Walk-forward: year 1 is used for indicator warm-up, years 2–3 are the out-of-sample test window reported above.
  • Single-instrument: results do not account for portfolio-level diversification or correlation effects.
